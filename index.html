Research

The majority of my research has focused on star clusters in the Milky Way and their evolution. Of particular interest to me are binary stars and their formation and evolution within star clusters. I use statistical analyses to study the properties of star forming regions. I use numerical simulations to constrain properties which can then be compared against observations.

My numerical simulations make use of the The Astrophysical Multipurpose Use Software Environment (AMUSE), which can be found on GitHub.

Currently I am developing a pipeline to automatically extract hundreds of known open clusters from Gaia DR2 using machine learning.

Data analysis of star forming regions

Three-dimensional scatter plot of the Hyades cluster, extracted using Gaussian Mixture Models from the raw data in the Gaia DR2 dataset. These points are sized by their absolute magnitude (Gaia G-Mag) and colored by their Gaia (G - RP) color. With the advent of massive surveys like the Gaia mission it is now possible to study stellar evolution in open clusters such as the Hyades in all three dimensions.

Plot of results from simulated observations of spectroscopic binaries using the time-baselines, and observational cadences from data produced by the IN-SYNC survey team. Plots like this one were used to evaluate the effectiveness of detection of spectroscopic binaries.

Numerical simulations of star clusters

This is a plot of mass fraction vs number fraction for a simulated star cluster at T=0 Myr and T=1000 Myr. The gini coefficient value is a measure of mass segregation (How much the high mass stars have migrated to the center of mass of the cluster).

This is an animation of a simple star cluster (red dots) in orbit (blue dashed line) around a Milky Way type galaxy center (+ symbol). As the star cluster orbits around this gravitational source, it slowly gets stretched out. This is known as tidal stripping and is a known mechanism for the dissolution of small star clusters.

This is a close-up animation of a star cluster orbiting around a Milky Way type galaxy. The stars within the star cluster are stripped (red dots) by the gravitational forces of the Milky Way. The star cluster (blue dots) evaporates over time. This affects the dynamical evolution of binary stars within the star cluster.

About

I grew up in Florida where I enjoyed the ease of access to the beaches and the temperate Florida weather. I recieved my bachelor's of science degree in Astronomy from the University of Florida, after which I worked as a server at several bars in Gainesville, FL. In 2015, I started my master's thesis through the Fisk-Vanderbilt Bridge program, which I successful defended in 2017. Currently I am a Ph.D student at the Vanderbilt University Astronomy Department.

When I am not working on my astronomy projects I will be either working on my 30 year old motorcycle or playing with my two cats (Try to find a picture of each on this website).
